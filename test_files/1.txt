In The Great Gatsby, F. Scott Fitzgerald exposes the hollow nature of the American Dream through Jay Gatsby’s relentless pursuit of wealth and status as a means to win Daisy’s love. Despite Gatsby’s lavish parties and grand displays of material success, his ultimate failure to recreate the past underscores the tragic futility of basing one’s identity on social standing and superficial desires. This paper will argue that Fitzgerald uses symbols like the green light, the valley of ashes, and Gatsby’s own origin story to highlight how dreams built on illusion inevitably crumble, revealing the moral bankruptcy at the heart of the Roaring Twenties.
In F. Scott Fitzgerald’s novel The Great Gatsby, the emptiness of the American Dream becomes evident through Jay Gatsby’s single-minded quest for wealth and prestige in hopes of rekindling his relationship with Daisy. Although Gatsby hosts extravagant parties and flaunts his apparent success, his inability to reclaim the past underscores how grounding one’s identity in social status and materialistic goals leads to downfall. This paper argues that Fitzgerald utilizes icons such as the green light, the valley of ashes, and Gatsby’s backstory to illustrate how aspirations built on illusion collapse, spotlighting the moral decay underlying the prosperity of the Roaring Twenties.
